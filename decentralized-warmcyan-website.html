Decentralized or Offline warmcyan.eco

A cool feature of this website (in my opinion!) is that all pages are static and all page links are relative to each other. There’s no server-side code that needs to run, and no CDNs that need to be hit to download gigabytes of JavaScript libraries for you to view it. For the non-technologically-inclined, this just means that the technology required to run this website is extremely simple. All the files are freely available, and you can very easily just download the whole site and browse it locally on your computer! Or for those who are more technically inclined, it’s pretty straightforward to host/mirror yourself with just a few commands.

Why?!

“Why on earth would I want to do that?” you exclaim.

Please indulge me in this very reasonable, very serious, and really quite plausible scenario: imagine that you’re out in a cabin in the middle of the woods. You’ve just gotten into an argument with your friend about whether the Planet Xchange thrift shop listed in The Knoxville Index is located on Kingston Pike or Cedar Bluff. Google Maps is currently not working, you blame the cabin WiFi. Your friend has an iPhone and tries using whatever maps app thing Apple has, but in a freak accident last night, cosmic radiation from a solar storm simultaneously struck every hard drive in Apple’s servers that happened to contain the location information for the Planet Xchange in Knoxville, and it just keeps referring you to some weird “null island” thing now. To make matters WORSE, it doesn’t even look like the warmcyan.eco website is running, because unfortunately a meteorite landed on the power pole right outside my house, taking my wonderful server offline. This is obviously more likely than you just not having internet or cell service out in the woods.

Anyway, spurred on by an insatiable need to prove your friend wrong, you remember that previous-you was smart and followed level 1 protocols listed below – you have a copy of this very website on your laptop! You pull it out, boot it up, open the knoxville.html page in your web browser (how cool, it looks exactly like as if you really were on warmcyan.eco!), and triumphantly show your friend that the aforementioned thrift shop is indeed on Kingston. Nice!

Again but with less dumb

More seriously, decentralization (some interesting wikipedia links: decentralization, decentralized web) vs centralization is a really important concept both in technology and in life more broadly. There are pros and cons to both, but I’m going to claim that more stuff on the decentralized side is often better – it mitigates issues regarding control/power, as well as reliability.

Spotify and Netflix are two examples of highly centralized services. In each case, one company has total centralized control of your media: you have to go through their systems and services to access the media that they own. On the one hand, this has some obvious benefits. For a monthly subscription fee, you’re granted access to massive catalogs, and there’s no resource requirements placed on you, e.g. storage space, either physical (CD’s, albums, DVD’s) or digital (hard drive space). This is overall more convenient and cheaper if your goal is to consume large quantities of diverse stuff! The downside is that you have zero control over “your” media. Netflix has the ability to remove (and often does) anything from their servers at will. I’m sure most people have at least one example of a good show getting moved to a different streaming platform they didn’t have a subscription for!

The decentralized version of this is to buy music/shows/movies in physical (or digital download) form. It doesn’t matter if the original source of something you own a copy of gets removed or deleted, you have your own local version of it! You can let a friend borrow it without Netflix getting upset that you’re sharing your password with someone. You can play it on lots of different hardware and software, without being stuck with Spotify’s interface. You fundamentally have control over your own media and aren’t even dependent on an internet connection in this scenario.

So why allow decentralization of this website?

In part, to experiment with and demonstrate that it’s possible. Not every website is (or even should be) dirt simple, but there are plenty (personal blogs, wiki’s, etc.) that could be if people wanted! My goal is to highlight that you can create content, information, and knowledge for other people in a way where they aren’t exclusively dependent on your (or anyone’s) server being up for them to access it. If even one other person grabs an offline version of this website (or better yet hosts it themselves for others), the information on this site lives on regardless of if my raspberry pi crashes or my internet goes down! The site can be “hosted” via sneakernet if need be! Similarly from the consumption side, my goal is to highlight that you can own significant quantities of information completely locally and offline, again without depending on internet or other people’s/company’s infrastructure (for a super interesting example of this, take a gander at kiwix).

There are certainly other means of doing this that rely on more technical solutions (e.g. IPFS, freenet) but the approaches listed below require only “standard” tools (based on level). The biggest downside to following them is namely the fact that the content isn’t “live”. If I make a change to one of the pages on this site, the next time you access it via the warmcyan.eco URL, you’ll see those new changes. If you access your local version, those changes won’t be there, so you have to manually go and fetch the new content based on the level.

A random fun thing enabled by having your own copy of this website is search! Since these are static pages, I don’t have any search functionality directly integrated into the website, but if you download the files you can use whatever search utility (e.g. the windows file explorer’s search function) to find the page or text you’re looking for.

How?

Level 1 - I have used a computer before

No more knowledge is necessary than that to download and access an offline copy of this website!

The raw files for this website are (currently) mirrored on github. To download a zip of those files, click this link: https://github.com/WarmCyan/warmcyan.eco/archive/refs/heads/main.zip.

Once you have the zip file downloaded, just unzip it (on windows you can probably right click on it and “extract” or “extract all files here” etc.), and click on the newly extracted index.html. This should open the homepage in your browser and you’re good to go!

(Alas this doesn’t work well on a phone, at least on android most browsers open up raw HTML files in a way that doesn’t let them access other local files.)

Level 2 - I know what git is

It’s like level 1 but somehow easier! Run:

git clone https://github.com/WarmCyan/warmcyan.eco.git

and click on index.html. Done!

This approach also makes it quicker to update your local version when I make changes, the new hot stuff fresh off the press is one git pull away.

Level 3 - I can run a python command

If you want to get fancy and have a python environment, you can minimally re-host your own version of this website by following either level 1 or level 2 to get the files locally and then using python’s nifty simple server by navigating to the files and running:

python -m http.server 8989

which will spin up a web server hosting the files at port 8989!

See the http.server docs for more info on options you can use.

(this is all built-in stuff, no pip installs necessary)

This does work in termux on android if you’re so inclined.

Level 4 - I have my own server

You really probably don’t need my suggestions for this if you’ve gotten this far and are somehow seriously considering it, and I’m too lazy to go grab the NGINX server block I have on my pi right now. But, at some point I’ll put a full setup with cron job to regularly re-pull the git stuff.
